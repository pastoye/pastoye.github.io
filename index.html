Photojournal


Most people have heard about the “hot car baby deaths” plastered on the headlines, those tragic stories of healthy, smiling toddlers dying suddenly and unexpectedly. I heard about it first when I was about 12 years old; that time, it was two little boys left in a heated car in the middle of winter in my town in western Colorado. Now I am 21: and that statistic has not decreased, nor even plateaued—it has increased, both in 2016 and 2017.


Why?


When I started working on Chirp, we went around and asked parents what they thought. Everyone cared about the issue, felt truly saddened by the deaths. But there was also always the looming question,


“How could the parents do that to their child?”


Here’s the answer: parents are superheroes, but they’re still human. When a parent is tired, stressed, trying to juggle several events for the afternoon, and suddenly a work emergency comes up, she might drive to work thinking that she already dropped off her little one at daycare, who is actually sleeping in the back. Or maybe a parent who usually doesn’t take his toddler to daycare takes the child while is wife is home with a cold, and forgets to make the stop, taking his normal route to the train and parking the car outside.


In fact, these were real parents, and real children. The first is a CEO of a Midwest hospital; the second, a near-miss, was a dad in the Boston area. 52% of cases are accidental, and there are no correlations with socioeconomic status.

It’s easy to think that those tragedies always happen to the “other” - to think, "that would never happen to me." But, the sad truth is, it can happen to anyone.



I want to make a safer world for my future children, and all future children. I want for parents to focus on their baby’s first words, about the six events they have to juggle later in the day, about their work—not on the potential for little mistakes to become a tragedy.


That’s why I believe it’s important that we start to accept that vehicular heatstroke can happen to anyone. A first step in that direction is the Hot Cars Act of 2017, a bi-partisan act which requires life-saving technology in every new vehicle. To me, this is both about instituting a simple, low-cost, preventative measure in cars, and acknowledging it’s a real safety issue that needs to be solved.


I’m working to get my local congressmen on board with this act. A quick call or email to your local congressmen’s office, or even telling your friends about the Act, would help to create a world where a child never dies in this preventable way.







In memory of William and Tyler Jensen, who died at ages 2 and 4 in their mother's car. 2009, Grand Junction, Colorado.
